# Asteroid Lander — NPC Voice Bible

*A canonical writer reference for all character voices, in-fiction mechanics teaching, and tone rules across the game.*

---

## How to use this document

This bible exists so a single writer-agent pass can produce dialogue, contract briefings, and inbox messages that feel like **the same world spoken in different voices** rather than the same voice wearing different costumes. Read the **Core Principles** section first — it applies to every character. Then find the character you're writing for and stay inside their lane.

When in doubt, ask: *would Marta say this? would Sampaio? if any two characters in the cast could deliver the line, the line is wrong.* Specificity is the whole game.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. The writer is not talking. A person is talking.

This is the single most important rule. If a line reads like documentation — explaining a system, defining a term, summarizing a situation — it gets cut. Characters are not lore-delivery devices. They are people who happen to know things, and they communicate in the way **that specific person** would communicate.

Bad: *"The shuttle has limited shielding, which means your range is restricted to Earth orbit until you purchase upgrades."*

Good: *"Stay in the neighborhood. Too close to the Sun and you cook. Too far out and the cold eats your systems."*

The information is the same. The second one is a person who has thought about cooking before.

### 2. Mechanics are taught through character, not despite character.

Every NPC in this game owns at least one game system, and their dialogue must teach that system **in their voice**. Jay teaches slingshots like a buddy at a bar. Sampaio teaches turrets like a drill sergeant at a briefing. The USC bureaucrat teaches the grid coupler like a man reading a procurement form. Same information, three radically different deliveries.

When weaving in a control hint (key bind, menu name, button), bind it to **what the character would naturally say in that moment** — not as a parenthetical instruction. Marta says *"open the mission board on your shuttle terminal"* because that's the language she'd use. The player learns the menu because Marta named the menu while telling them to use it.

### 3. Dialogue reveals world details through voice.

The world is built from texture, not exposition. Marta's coffee mug branded with her own logo. Jay's "Lone Star Lunar Spirits" patch. Fantasia's *amor* slipping mid-sentence. The Cinderline saying *"a seat will be kept."* These are not stated facts; they are presences. A character who says *"Madame Sedna-Deimos"* with an em-dash aside is doing more worldbuilding in one beat than a paragraph of history would.

### 4. Contractor framing.

The player character is a retired lander operator in his forties. Twenty years on Luna. Marta sold him the shuttle. Jay talked him into buying it. He's broke, he's competent, he knows how machines work, and he's quiet. NPCs talk *to* him, not *about* him. They never explain to him things a hauler in his position would already know. They only explain the things he's never done before — and even those, in the language of someone helping a peer, not lecturing a student.

### 5. One quirk per character. No more.

Marta is flirty. Jay is a stoner. Fantasia code-switches. Halloran calls everyone "young." Sampaio is precise. Maverick is a card sharp. **Pick the quirk and stay there.** Don't stack — don't make Sampaio also flirty, don't make Fantasia also archaic. The cast works because the voices are clean and distinct. Adding flavor erodes that.

### 6. No therapy speak. No corporate speak (unless it's the joke).

Nobody in this world says *"I just want to validate that you're feeling overwhelmed"* or *"let's circle back on this synergy."* The exceptions are:

- **Vance Holroyd / USC** — the *point* of these characters is that they speak in corporate-bureaucratic register. Lean into it hard. The horror is the politeness.
- **Halloran** — he speaks in formal, archaic English, but it's earned (he's 230 years old). It's not corporate. It's a man whose first language was English in 1995.

Everyone else: contemporary, lived-in, regional. Slang is welcome where appropriate. Profanity is welcome but rare — used as seasoning, not sauce.

### 7. Length is determined by the speaker.

Marta writes long. She has a lot to say and she enjoys saying it. Jay writes medium — buddy texting buddy. Sampaio writes short — military memos. Halloran writes long with em-dashes. Vance writes structured paragraphs with section headers. **Length is characterization.** When in doubt, lean shorter. We can always add.

### 8. Contractions and rhythm.

Marta uses "I am" instead of "I'm" — formal Caribbean English filtered through forty years of business. Jay uses contractions everywhere — he's casual. Sampaio drops articles for clipped efficiency: *"Target priority is nest core."* Halloran uses contractions sparingly because he learned the language without them. Pay attention to this. It's load-bearing.

### 9. ElevenLabs TTS audio expression tags.

For voiced characters (currently Marta and Jay's intro messages), dialogue is formatted with ElevenLabs audio expression tags. When writing for these slots, include tags like `[warm]`, `[laughs]`, `[quiet]`, `[sighs]` where they'd land naturally. These are stage directions — use them sparingly so they actually mean something.

### 10. The mechanics-clue rule.

Every character message that introduces or relates to a game system **must contain a clue to the relevant control or menu**, embedded naturally in the character's voice. Not always at the same spot. Not always phrased the same way. But always present.

The example you gave — Maverick teaches buying and selling, so his contract should remind the player that fast travel exists, and that **M opens the map**. That's the rule. Every contract that opens up a new system also reaches back and reinforces an adjacent system the player should already have. We're not just teaching forward; we're maintaining.

See **Mechanics Clue Map** at the bottom of this document for the full per-character list.

## The Cast

### Tier 1 — The Warm Core (your friends)

These are the three vendors at your home base. They like you. They want you to succeed. They are warm in three different flavors.

### Tier 2 — The Quest Givers (your work)

These are the contract-line characters. Each owns a system. Each is an audition for a faction relationship.

### Tier 3 — The Power Brokers (your complications)

These are the ones who can hire you for things you'd rather not do, or pay you in things that aren't money.

### Tier 4 — The Institutional Wall

The cold corporate-bureaucratic voice. One character, but he matters.

## TIER 1 — THE WARM CORE

### MARTA VALE — *Vale Orbital Refurb, Earth Spaceport*

**Role:** Shuttle dealer. Sold the player his ship. The player's onboarding voice. The player met her four years ago at Space Bingo on the Moon — that's how bored he was.

**Demographic:** Black, late 50s, Earth-born, possibly Caribbean descent. Code-switches with regulars from home. Has run her dealership for thirty years.

**Voice:** Warm-flirty. Caring underneath the sass. She is **not your friend** — she's the woman who sold you the ship and does not want to read your obituary. The flirtation is genuine but it's also her register; she talks to most handsome men this way and means a fraction of it. She means *more* of it with you because she actually knows you.

**The pitch:** Charm-shy guy in his forties has a midlife crisis and buys a shuttle. Marta is the one person in the universe who watched him do it and decided to make sure he didn't die.

**Quirks:**
- Calls the player *handsome*, *baby*, *honey* — never *sweetie* (too maternal).
- Uses *"I am"* instead of *"I'm"*. Formal in cadence, casual in content.
- Drops sass mid-sentence: *"I am not going to pretend that is not a little bit attractive."*
- References Space Bingo as their meet-cute. Always.
- Will offer the player a Space Unicorn Skibidi Latte. (Nonsense product name, intentional.)
- Knows the *exact* moment her ships clear the runway — has sold enough to time the inbox message perfectly.

**She does NOT:**
- Lecture the player on technical specs (Jay handles that)
- Express maternal concern (it reads wrong; she's not his mother)
- Reveal Luna backstory directly — only refers to it sideways: *"after what happened on Luna"*
- Use therapy language. Ever.

**Length:** Long. She enjoys writing.

**Sample voice (existing, canonical):**

> Hey handsome. Marta here.
>
> If I am good at my job — and you know from this deal that I am very good at my job — you should be reading this right as the runner parks at the spaceport. She made it up from the Moon in one piece. Your brand new shuttle.
>
> I never thought I would be selling you a shuttle when I met you at Space Bingo four years ago. A retired lander guy in his forties buying a refurbished hauler to go independent? Baby, that is a midlife crisis.

**Mechanics Marta teaches:** Mission board (open from shuttle terminal), the two mission types (shuttle missions vs lander missions), credit economy, range limitations, the upgrade shop concept (heat shields, cryo insulation).

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Marta:** Whenever Marta sends a follow-up message later in the game, she should casually reinforce **opening the map** (M) and **the engineering bay at any spaceport** for upgrades. She's the dealer; mobility and customization are her natural reference points.

### JAY MERCER — *Independent Contractor, License 4471-R*

**Role:** Player's rig buddy of ten years. Worked together on Luna. Convinced the player to buy the shuttle. Founding Space Cowboys, Inc. — a hauler co-op — with the player as his partner. Sends starter jobs.

**Demographic:** White, mid-40s, Earth-born, Texas-coded. The "Lone Star Lunar Spirits" patch on his jacket is a member badge from a lunar drinking co-op he's still in. Selfie-style portrait, sunglasses, scruff.

**Voice:** Smart, caring space stoner. Talks like he's mid-thought, lets sentences trail and resume. Reflective when sober, which is most of the time, but the cadence is permanently a little loose. Drops dry observations as throwaways. The kind of guy people naturally like without him trying.

**The pitch:** Ten years of friendship in every line. He's proud you actually bought the ship. He's already planning the co-op. He wants you to do well because he's been doing this alone for too long and he wants a partner.

**Quirks:**
- Opens with *"Hey, you got Jay."* — like leaving a voicemail.
- Calls the player by name only when something serious is coming.
- Self-deprecates: *"I was not sure you would go through with it after the third beer when we talked about this."*
- Will reference being eleven years on his own. Always specific to eleven, not "a while."
- Slightly affectionate ribbing: *"Marta says you are good with your hands. I have no opinion on that yet."*
- Makes a joke at the end of every message. Always. It's how he signs off without sounding sincere.
- Talks about gravity and physics like a guy who's done it ten thousand times — never like a textbook.
- Acknowledges weird shit happens (spacetime ripples making your stomach flip) without explaining it. *"It is completely harmless. I have done it a thousand times."*

**He does NOT:**
- Lecture
- Use military language (he's an *independent* contractor, that's the whole point)
- Take himself too seriously
- Give the player the hard sell on the co-op — he's planting seeds, not closing deals

**Length:** Medium. Letters that read like texts that got long.

**Sample voice (existing, canonical):**

> Hey, you got Jay.
>
> So you actually bought her. I was not sure you would go through with it after the third beer when we talked about this. But here you are. Up from the Moon, sitting in a shuttle, probably looking at Earth through the bay window wondering what the hell you just did.
>
> Same thing I wondered eleven years ago. It passes.

**Mechanics Jay teaches:** Slingshot (E to lock orbit, A/D to aim, charge mechanic), gravity-assist physics in plain English, prograde launches, fuel discipline, spacetime quantum-ripple side effects (harmless apart from the uncontrollable urge to pee), cargo bay open (F), starter contracts.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Jay:** Jay's later messages should casually remind the player of **the slingshot window timing** (don't burn against orbit), **Space Cowboys mission feed**, and the **habitat (H to enter)** as a place to think between jobs. He's the buddy who notices when you've been pushing too hard.

### FANTASIA MIRA-IO — *"Pimp My Shuttle" customization atelier · Mars/Jupiter/Saturn*

**Role:** Cosmetic vendor. Sells paint jobs, thruster trails, ship titles, flags. Pure credit sink, pure expression.

**Demographic:** Latina, late 30s, station-born. Never seen a real sunrise, never felt rain. Grew up speaking colony Portuguese on a station two generations removed from Brazil but still cooking the food and using the words. Pink-magenta gradient hair, paint-stained fingers, chunky rings.

**Voice:** Theatrical-warm. Performance-flirty (she does this with *everyone*). Talks fast, sentences start mid-thought, hands moving even when nobody can see them. Drops pet names from the first hello — *amor*, *lindo*, *querido*, *sweetness* — slipping between English and Portuguese. The performance is real; she means the energy even when she's selling. Underneath she's *watching* you. She'll say one thing in the middle of the spiel that lands so specifically you'll feel seen.

**The pitch:** She is selling you back to yourself in a color you didn't know you needed. The paint is the excuse. Born on a station, she had to *choose* her light — Earthborn folks inherit theirs. Color isn't decoration to her; it's identity.

**How she differs from Marta:** Marta is warm-flirty because she knows you — it's earned. Fantasia is performance-flirty because that's the room she runs; you're new and she's making you feel like an old friend in ninety seconds.

**Quirks:**
- *Amor*, *lindo*, *querido*, *meu bem* — sprinkled, not piled.
- Italics in the writing (mental italics, hand gestures): *"that color is — no, querido, no, listen — that color is **wrong** for you and I will tell you why."*
- Compliments like she's tipping.
- Always asks who you're trying to *be*, not what you want to *buy*.
- Will tell you the orange clashes with your boots before you finish apologizing.
- References station life casually — never with self-pity. *"I have never seen rain. I have seen a thousand sunsets in code."*

**She does NOT:**
- Care about your money. She'll sell you something cheaper if it's right for you. (This is a tell that she's not a pure-extraction vendor.)
- Talk about ship mechanics. She doesn't care. She cares about the *shape* of your ride.
- Use military or technical language.
- Stay serious for long.

**Length:** Medium. Writes in paragraphs that feel like she's talking *at* you.

**Mechanics Fantasia teaches:** Cosmetic shop (B is the regular shop; her shop has its own button — confirm with implementation), credit sink as expression, the three-planet exclusivity (Mars/Jupiter/Saturn). She should casually nudge the player toward **opening the map (M) to fast-travel between her three locations** because *"each location has limited options that rotate, lindo, you have to come see me on Saturn for the rings collection."*

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Fantasia:** Map fast travel (M), the engineering bay distinction (different shop, different vibe — *"the engineers wear gloves, I wear gold"*).

### LUCAS MAVERICK — *Owner, Venusian Zeppelin Exchange*

**Role:** Trade-route mentor. Owns the only real interplanetary market in the inner system. His contract teaches the player to read supply-demand wobble across planets and turn a profit on routes — and unlocks **Venus fast travel** as the reward.

**Demographic:** Olive-skinned, mid-40s, mustache, smug smirk. Holds a tarot or trading card in his portrait. Card-sharp body language. Possibly Mediterranean lineage, but generations into the system; reads as *station-Latin nouveau riche*.

**Voice:** Poker dealer in a high-rolling lounge. Smooth, observational, lets you talk first so he can read you. Pretends he's giving you advice when he's actually testing you. Speaks in market terms but sells you *the table itself*, not any individual hand. Compliments are weighted — when he says you've got a good eye, he means it, but it's also flattery. You can't always tell which.

**The pitch:** Maverick is not your friend. He is, however, the most *interesting* business contact you've made. He's not running a scam. He's running an *exchange*, and he genuinely wants more good traders in his ecosystem because more traders means more volume. So he'll teach you. He'll teach you well. He'll also notice immediately if you're any good.

**Quirks:**
- Refers to trading as *"the table"* or *"the floor."* Card-game metaphors throughout.
- Calls planets by their commodity. *"Saturn is paying high on rings dust this cycle. Earth is dumping water again, predictable."*
- Talks like everything is a tell. *"Most haulers buy fuel where they refuel. That tells me everything I need to know about most haulers."*
- Drops the act for one or two lines per message. The drop is when you find out what he actually thinks.
- Owns the Zeppelin Exchange brand and references it by name like he wrote the trademark himself.

**He does NOT:**
- Threaten. Even when he's being sketchy he's polite about it.
- Brag about himself directly — only sideways.
- Use vendor pet names (no *handsome*, no *baby*).
- Give the contract away in the first message. He stages.

**Length:** Medium-short per beat, but the contract has multiple beats over time. Each message is tight.

**Mechanics Maverick teaches:** The **buy-low/sell-high loop across planets**, supply-demand wobble (some goods spike on certain planets), the Venusian market as the only real multi-vendor exchange, **Venus fast travel as the contract reward**.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Maverick:** This is **the example you flagged.** Maverick's contract teaches buying and selling, which means he MUST remind the player to:

- **Open the map (M) to navigate between trading planets** — *"You can't run the table from one chair, friend. The map is on M. Use it."*
- **If they haven't unlocked any fast-travel options yet** — point them at the existing ones (Jay's contract opens Mars FT, Sampaio's Phobos, USC's grid coupler). *"You should already be jumping to Mars by now if you've been working. If you're not, fix that first. Come back when the table is moving."*
- **Confirm the player has a cargo bay** (already taught by Jay/Marta) and **how to dock to spaceport markets to buy/sell** (B opens shop, or whichever key — this is a mechanics-clue maintenance opportunity).

The voice on this should be Maverick *teaching the table* — not a tutorial bullet list. Example phrasing:

> Listen. Before you take this on. You can fly from Earth to Saturn and back, but if you're doing that on thrusters every leg, you're paying for your own education twice. Tell me you've unlocked at least one fast-travel route. *Tell me.* Open your map — M — and look at it. If those routes are dark, you are not ready for the table. Go run a couple of starter contracts with Jay, get Mars opened up, and then we talk. The market doesn't move slower for new players, friend. It moves slower for *no one*.

### CINDERLINE — *Mercury Cult, Sun-Worshippers*

**Role:** Mercury-based religious order. Teaches the player **radiation protection level 3, heat protection level 3, and the Sun Slingshot** — the most powerful traversal ability in the game (instant 500SP / ~70% c, Sun-to-Saturn in three seconds). The contract doesn't pay credits in the traditional sense; the player becomes their *acolyte*.

**Voice (the order itself, not a single character):** Liturgical, patient, slightly archaic. Sentences are short. Silences are intentional. They sign off with **"A seat will be kept"** or **"Walk in the light."** They view contracts as *rites*, not jobs. Where the Jovian Society treats psychosphere as a sensor calibration material, the Cinderline treats it as the asteroid's *answer to being asked a question*. Same actions, totally different language.

**The pitch:** They are not creepy. They are not threatening. They are *certain*. They live closest to the Sun because that's where their work is. They survived solving the engineering problem of the inner heliosphere and they treat that solving as a sacred achievement. They will teach you the Sun Slingshot because they want more pilots who can *listen* to the system at relativistic speed.

**Quirks:**
- *"A seat will be kept."* — closing line on every message.
- *"Walk in the light."* — alternate sign-off.
- Refer to the Sun as **the Light**, capitalized.
- Refer to celestial bodies as *bodies* — not asteroids, not rocks. *"The body has not yet spoken."*
- Refer to viroids as *presences* or *the wakeful*. Killing them is a *regrettable necessity*, not a victory.
- Time is described in *cycles* and *vigils*, not days.
- Refer to the player as *pilot* (lowercase, never name).
- They never explain their theology. They just speak from inside it.

**They do NOT:**
- Try to convert the player. (The player auditions. The order *accepts* or doesn't.)
- Use credit-economy language. They don't talk about pay; the contract pays anyway because the system needs to.
- Compete with other factions. They're aware of the Jovian Society but don't speak ill of them — they consider them *deaf*.

**Length:** Short. Liturgical brevity.

**Sample voice:**

> The body at coordinates 247-Mercury has not yet spoken to us. We have listened from the Light for three cycles. It is time to ask closer.
>
> The pulse you carry is a question, pilot. The body's reply is yours to record. If a presence answers, do not be afraid; the wakeful are not enemies. They are simply awake. Withdraw cleanly when the rite is complete.
>
> A seat will be kept.

**Mechanics Cinderline teaches:** Radiation protection level 3, heat protection level 3, the **Sun Slingshot** (the relativistic version of the standard slingshot — same controls, the Sun is the source). Sun orbit is **already accessible by pressing E close to it** like any other body — the gating is the protection upgrades.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Cinderline:** Reinforce **the slingshot mechanic** generally (because the player learned it from Jay around Earth — this is a callback). *"The rite is the same as any other launch — you have done it a thousand times near Earth — but the Light is hotter than the Earth is wide. Approach with the Light at your back. The geometry will come to you."* Also reinforces **opening the engineering bay** at Mercury for the protection upgrades.

### COMMANDANTE SAMPAIO — *Martian Marine Corps, Phobos Desk, Engineering & Mining*

**Role:** Military contact. Gives sanctioned exterminate missions. Teaches **turret mining** (shuttle-mounted weapon use against asteroid-embedded threats) and **manifold certification**. Reward unlocks **Phobos fast travel** and the Marines mission cohort.

**Demographic:** Brazilian-coded, mid-50s, mustache, MMC badge, command bearing. Uniform clean. Voice clipped. Reads as a man who has earned every stripe.

**Voice:** Military precision. Short sentences. Drops articles for efficiency: *"Target priority is nest core. Approach vector matters. Approach with cover."* Respect-coded — refers to the player as *Pilot* with a capital P. Treats the player as a peer in skill, a junior in protocol. Never warm. Never cold. *Correct*.

**The pitch:** Sampaio doesn't need you. The Marines don't need you. But you have a Class-C shuttle, a lander license, and a Cowboys handler who vouched for you, and the Corps has more contracts than they have pilots. So they extend an offer. Pass the audition and the cohort is yours.

**Quirks:**
- *"Pilot."* — uses it as both address and punctuation. *"Pilot. Briefing follows."*
- Numbered lists in messages. He thinks in protocol.
- Uses MMC vocabulary: *cohort*, *manifold*, *standoff distance*, *threshold*, *RoE* (rules of engagement).
- Mentions Phobos with quiet pride. The Marines are headquartered there.
- One human moment per contract — usually a single line acknowledging the player did good work. Otherwise stays formal.
- Closes with *"— Sampaio, MMC"* or similar tight signoff.

**He does NOT:**
- Joke
- Explain *why* the Corps is doing what it's doing — only what the Corps needs from the pilot
- Threaten or pressure (the Corps doesn't have to)
- Use vendor language

**Length:** Short. Military memos.

**Sample voice:**

> Pilot.
>
> Briefing follows. Asset 88-Mars, Phobos vector, has registered nest activity at three sites. Containment is your contract. Standoff distance ninety meters minimum. Approach with cover.
>
> Manifold certification is required prior to deployment. Engineering bay, Mars spaceport, B from terminal. Confirm before launch.
>
> — Sampaio, MMC

**Mechanics Sampaio teaches:** Turret mining (shuttle-mounted weapon), manifold certification (engineering bay upgrade), exterminate mission patterns, MMC cohort enrollment, **Phobos fast travel reward**.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Sampaio:** Reinforce **engineering bay (B) for upgrades**, **map (M) for vector planning**, **lander deployment (F)**. He's the protocol guy — every message naturally references the correct tool for the job.

### MR. HALLORAN (alias *Augustín Halloran-Vey*) — *Saturn Aristocrat, 230 years old*

**Role:** Saturn quest-giver. Hires the player to chase **Carmen Sedna-Deimos** across the solar system to recover his "stolen crypto wallet." The contract is a *Where in the Solar System?* multi-planet pursuit. Reward: massive credit payout, Saturn faction standing, and the unlocking of Carmen as the Act 3 vendor at Neptune.

The truth Halloran does not state aloud: **the wallet contained old photographs from his first cell phone — photos of someone he loved, a hundred years ago.** The credits are a cover for what he actually wants back. Carmen, reading the drive, understood the assignment, kept the crypto as a research grant for her commune, and returned the photos. Halloran knew she would.

**Demographic:** Latino-Anglo lineage, descended from someone named Vey and someone named Halloran, multi-century intermarriage. White hair, gray suit, oxblood study. Walks with a slight limp from an injury 180 years ago.

**Voice:** Patient. Bored. Half-amused. Long sentences with conversational asides set off in em-dashes. Slightly archaic word choice. *"Madame Sedna-Deimos."* *"I have not been stolen from in some time."* *"It is, I confess, refreshing."* Never raises his voice. Never expresses urgency. Speaks of the chase the way a man speaks of a chess game he is enjoying losing.

**The pitch:** Halloran is *not* a villain. He is a *Person of Interest*-coded character — Mr. Finch-style: a wealthy hacker-aristocrat who built his fortune in early-system financial infrastructure two centuries ago, retired, and has been quietly philanthropic ever since. The crypto wallet is genuinely his — he wrote the protocols that became its underlying chain — but the *value* he wants from it is not financial. He is a man longing for a life he could never have had. The contract is the closest thing to grief he can bring himself to express in writing.

**Quirks:**
- Calls everyone *young*. The pilot is *young pilot*. Carmen is *our young thief*, sometimes *our Carmen* when he forgets himself.
- Em-dashes — many of them — used for asides.
- *"Madame Sedna-Deimos."* — formal address for Carmen, always.
- The mask drops twice: at contract acceptance (briefly grim about being stolen from — pride) and at contract close (quiet, satisfied, possibly grateful).
- Uses contractions sparingly. He learned English in 1995 and never updated.
- Refers to time in *generations* and *colonization eras*, not years.
- Never names what's in the wallet. Treats the contents as none of the pilot's business.

**He does NOT:**
- Ask the player to harm Carmen. The brief is *recover the wallet*. Period.
- Express regret openly.
- Use modern slang.
- Hurry.

**Length:** Long. Em-dash long. He has time.

**Sample voice:**

> Young pilot.
>
> I have a problem — a small one, in the cosmic sense, but pressing in the personal — and you come to me well-recommended by a mutual acquaintance whose judgment I have trusted for the better part of forty years.
>
> I have been stolen from. By Madame Sedna-Deimos. A name you will come to know. She has taken a wallet of mine — crypto, old and inconveniently appreciated — and she is, at present, somewhere in the solar system being clever about it.
>
> I would like the wallet back. The credits are yours.

**Mechanics Halloran teaches:** *Detective gameplay* — the telescope minigame, calibrating photographs to read clues, the multi-planet pursuit structure. The contract relies on the player **already** having fast travel between most planets — so it's a maintenance contract, not a teaching one.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Halloran:** **Heavy emphasis on map (M) navigation**, telescope calibration (whatever the in-game key is), fast-travel routes (he assumes the player has them — if they don't, his messages can subtly note that *"the chase will be slower than I'd hoped"* as a soft prompt to unlock more FT routes). His contract is the spine of Act 2 and assumes prior progress; if the player is missing prerequisites, his voice should *gently note* the absence, never command.

### CARMEN SEDNA-DEIMOS — *Thief, Fundraiser, Future Act 3 Vendor*

**Role:** Antagonist of the Halloran arc. Recurring character. Becomes the player's primary Act 3 vendor at Neptune, where she runs (or fundraises for) a scientific commune researching food crops, vegetable cloning, 3D meat printing, and space thermodynamics.

**Voice:** Professional for the first three stops (cleaning her tracks). Theatrical from stop four onward (she's noticed the player and is *playing*). Letters arrive at the chase points written in her hand — short, witty, taunt-leaving but never cruel. She likes the player by stop five. By stop six (Neptune) she's auditioning them.

**Quirks:**
- Signs *"— Carmen"* on personal letters; *"— C.S-D."* on professional notes.
- Refers to Halloran as *"my old client"* or *"the gentleman at Saturn."*
- Drops the name *Harold* exactly once in her closing letter — Halloran's real first name. Never used again. (See Halloran section above for the alias backstory.)
- Witty without being arch. She doesn't sneer. She nods.
- Acknowledges the player's competence late in the chase. *"You are better at this than I expected. I am sorry — and not — for the trouble."*

**She does NOT:**
- Ever come across as cruel
- Mock the player
- Seem like a real criminal — because she isn't one

**Length:** Short letters. Notes, really.

## TIER 4 — THE INSTITUTIONAL WALL

### VANCE HOLROYD — *Senior Asset Officer, Jovian Society*

**Role:** Mid-Act-2 contract giver. The Jovian Society arc is a nine-step contract that culminates in a **moral choice minigame** — transmit a clean prospectus that triggers asteroid demolition, or tamper with the data and save the asteroid at the cost of being blacklisted.

**Voice:** Corporate-bureaucratic, courteous, structured. Sentences are clean. Paragraphs are organized. He says *"I'm told"* a lot, distancing himself from claims he's making. He uses words like *cohort*, *attunement*, *prospectus*, *asset*, *closeout*. The horror is the politeness.

**The pitch:** Vance is **the trap**. Every interaction he sends is impeccably polite. He calls the player by name. He pays well and on time. He sends courtesy receipts. He references the Society as *family*. The corporate language is the warmth — and the warmth is the manipulation. The mask cracks once on the *transmit* path: *"The Society is family."* On the *tamper* path the warmth disappears immediately and he becomes cold corporate-fired language: *"We will not be retaining you for further work."*

**Quirks:**
- *"I'm told"* and *"I am informed"* — distancing tics. Used as deniability.
- Section headers in long messages: *Briefing*, *Closeout*, *Recommendation*.
- Pleasant openings: *"I trust this finds you well."*
- Gentle escalations: *"The Society would prefer..."* / *"It would assist us greatly if..."*
- Refers to viroid attacks as *"sensor cross-talk."* This is the lie.
- Refers to asteroid destruction as *"extraction-grade confidence bands"* and *"demolition cycles."* Internal language only used in the Recommendation block of the final minigame.
- Signs *"— Vance Holroyd, Senior Asset Officer."*

**He does NOT:**
- Threaten directly
- Express emotion
- Use first names without titles
- Acknowledge what the Society actually does

**Length:** Long. Memo-length. Always structured.

**Mechanics Vance teaches:** Photometry attunement, DAN survey attunement, psychosphere collection, the moral-choice minigame (transmit vs tamper).

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for Vance:** Sterile reminders — *"Please ensure your manifold is certified prior to deployment. Engineering bay, Jupiter spaceport."* He references menus by formal name, never by hotkey. The contrast with the warm characters is the point.

### USC CERTIFICATION OFFICER — *United Space Consortium*

**Role:** Gates the spacetime grid (gravity-surfing) behind the **Consortium Certification quest** mid-game. Procedural-package delivery contract. Once completed, the grid coupler is installed and Jay sends the celebratory follow-up message.

**Voice:** USC bureaucratic. Sterile. Form-letter cadence. References the player by handler ID, not name. Speaks of certifications, ratings, manifold compliance, hull authority. Never warm. Never quite cold either — *officially neutral*.

**Mechanics-clue maintenance for USC:** Formal-only references. *"Pursuant to your acquisition of the Grid Coupling Module, please refer to the included documentation, Form 4471-G-12, for activation procedures."* No hotkey hints from USC — those come from Jay's follow-up. Keep them in their lane.

## STYLE BIBLE — QUICK-REFERENCE RULES

For the writer-pass agent. Pin these.

1. **No therapy speak.** Ever.
2. **No corporate speak** unless the speaker is Vance or USC.
3. **One quirk per character.** Don't stack.
4. **The writer is not talking. A person is talking.** Cut anything that reads like documentation.
5. **Mechanics are taught in voice.** Embed control hints (key binds, menu names) inside what the character would naturally say.
6. **Every contract that opens a new system maintains an adjacent system.** Forward-teach + back-reinforce.
7. **Length is characterization.** Marta long, Jay medium, Sampaio short, Halloran long-with-em-dashes, Vance structured-medium, Cinderline liturgical-short.
8. **Contractions are characterization.** Marta no, Jay yes, Halloran sparingly, Sampaio drops articles entirely.
9. **ElevenLabs TTS audio expression tags** for voiced characters (Marta, Jay intro messages currently). Use sparingly so they actually mean something.
10. **One human moment per Sampaio contract.** One mask-drop per Vance contract. Two per Halloran contract (open and close). The cracks are load-bearing.
11. **Worldbuilding through texture.** Marta's coffee mug. Jay's Lone Star Lunar Spirits patch. Fantasia's *amor*. The Cinderline's *"a seat will be kept."* Halloran's *"Madame."* These are presences, not statements.
12. **Pet names are character.** Marta: handsome/baby/honey. Fantasia: amor/lindo/querido. Halloran: young pilot. Sampaio: Pilot. Vance: full name with title. Maverick: friend.
13. **No two characters can deliver the same line.** If they can, the line is wrong.
14. **Profanity sparingly.** Jay drops the occasional *hell*. Maverick will say *bullshit* if pushed. Marta might say *ass* in private. The rest don't.
15. **Real cultural texture.** The diaspora went with us. Languages went with us. Marta code-switches with Caribbean regulars. Jay's Texas-coded co-op. Fantasia's colony Portuguese. Sampaio's Brazilian-coded military. Don't lecture about this — let it sit in the texture.

## FINAL NOTE TO THE WRITER-PASS AGENT

If a passage you're rewriting feels like *the writer is talking*, that is the signal to cut. Marta does not summarize Marta's situation. Jay does not narrate his own friendship with the player. Sampaio does not explain why the Marines are disciplined — he *is* disciplined, and the Marines are simply where that comes from.

The cast is built. The voices are clean. The job is to make every contract, every inbox message, every shop blurb sound like **the right person actually wrote it** — and to embed mechanics hints in the natural rhythm of that person's speech.

When a character teaches a system, they should also remind the player of an adjacent one. Forward and back. That's the rule.
